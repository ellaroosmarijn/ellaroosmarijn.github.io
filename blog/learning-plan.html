A commitment to how you will manage your workload in this programme. This should include:
a) An explanation of how you will manage yourself to work productively and safely with other learners, Facilitators and industry/community representatives

I have some personal guidelines that I’ve formed over the years to help me navigate working in teams, on my own, in the workplace and in study spaces. They have helped me work respectively, productively, with honesty and integrity, and soak up the full benefit of the task.

  1. Ensure that I am always challenging myself, even if the challenge is small. Reflecting on my workload and regularly assessing where I’m at is important. It means that I can ask for help with a problem, or seek more of a challenge if I need it, to ensure my continual growth. I have found that David Goggins book “Can’t Hurt Me” has given me a lot of inspiration in this area.
  2. Upholding equality in workload according to each person's own capabilities. When studying in a group it is important to remember that dividing the workload evenly doesn’t mean that it is equal. The workload needs to be assessed according to each member's capabilities. This one is really important to keep in mind, since many people struggle with learning difficulties and invisible conditions which can make studying or working more of a challenge.
  3. To cultivate a good community based on honesty and respect.
  4. Giving credit where it is due, supporting each other and always voicing a compliment rather than keeping it to myself.
  5. Extreme ownership is something I’ve come to really value through Jocko Willink’s books, where he talks about the importance of extreme ownership. Extreme ownership is the idea that the leaders and each individual must own their mistakes. I think it is really important in groups that each individual recognises their mistakes and owns them, so they can learn from them and do better next time. Mistakes are just part of the learning process, and we’re co instantly learning.
  6. Remember to enjoy the process, and ups and downs of learning. I don’t want to look back one day and wish that I had spent more time enjoying the ups and downs of the moment, rather than just powering through. This is an important one for me, since I’m not one to slow down and be in the moment.
b) What you will do when things are building up and starting to get to you.

When the workload and pressure begins to build up, I take a step back and look at it more objectively. For me, this means recognising how much has been accomplished so far, and then breaking what needs to be done into smaller pieces. This technique comes back to a little mantra I use: “How do you eat an elephant? One bite at a time”. I find that it reminds me that everything doesn’t need to be done at once and that it's more sustainable to chip away at it piece by piece.

A commitment as to how and where you will seek help in a timely way.

I have found that my system for overcoming obstacles ensures that I seek help, if I need it, before I waste too much time on it. My system is as follows:

  1. Review my work to find the issue.
  2. Understand the issue - this may be a matter of googling or reviewing my notes.
  3. If there are multiple issues I will begin with the most fundamental issue.
  4. If I can’t resolve it at the time, then I will go back through my notes or google it.
  5. If this fails I will ask for help from a facilitator, my peers, or my partner.
  6. Once solved, I write the issue and solution in my notes to help commit it to memory and for future reference if the issue occurs again.
To ensure that I still don’t waste too much time, I will allocate a rough time limit to try and figure it out before asking for help. I adjust that time limit according to how big or complex of an issue I’m facing.
