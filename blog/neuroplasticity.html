Neuroplasticity and a Growth Mindset

Sprint Four

Neuroplasticity.

Neuroplasticity is the brain's ability to create, reform and strengthen neural pathways, and it is the process in which permanent learning takes place.

When you learn something new or form a new memory, the brain creates neural pathways connecting cells. It benefits people by enabling them to learn new things and ‘rewire’ their brain to adapt to new ideas, ways of living or to recover from injury. By doing, or even just thinking, you can strengthen these neural pathways. Studies have shown that by thinking about something, such as playing the piano, one can improve their ability to play at a similar (though slighter lesser) rate to those that physically practise. This is because thinking still ignites the neural pathways and strengthens it so that it will not be ‘broken’, or forgotten, easily. As the saying often goes “practice makes perfect”.

The five principles of of neuroplasticity are:

  1. Challenge and Novelty: It must be new and challenging.
  2. Intention: It must have meaning, relevance or importance.
  3. Specific Attention: It must focus on an exact tasks.
  4. Repetition: Repition and practise makes perfect.
  5. Time: Learning new things takes practise, patience and time.

Lets go into a little more depth on the principle of repetition. The idea behind repetition is that neural pathways that are not actively engaged will shrink and eventually break, so that you can no longer recall it. It must be actively engaged inorder to strengthen it. This is why repetition is so important.

A technique for aiding the process of committing something to your permanent memory is to link what you’ve just learned with something you already know. For instance, you may learn that the musical note c sharp (c#) creates a high pitched sound. If you link this new knowledge to your pre-existing that a sharp noise tends to be high pitched, you will find it easier to commit it to memory. This is because when one neural link is triggered, it can trigger other neural pathways too. Thus practice, repetition and linking new knowledge to older knowledge improves neuroplasticity.

A Growth Mindset.

A growth mindset is the belief that talents can be developed through hard work, good strategies and feedback. People with a growth mindset tend to put more emphasis on improving their skills, and thus achieve more than people with a fixed mindset. A fixed mindset is the belief that one’s abilities, intelligence and talents are fixed and cannot be improved upon. People with a fixed mindset tend to put their focus on trying to look smart, rather than on improving their skills.

Dwerk states that to achieve a growth mindset one must recognise that they also have a fixed mindset, because a pure growth mindset does not exist. To improve your growth mindset you must give praise and reward to yourself and others for effort, learning, progress and for the process you used to get there. An example of these processes would be seeking help from others, trying new things, and learning from setbacks. By integrating these things into your learning journey you can culture a growth mindset.
